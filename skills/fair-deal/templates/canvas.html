The Fair Deal Canvas

A planning canvas for any deal — partnership, co-founder, client, JV, advisory. Fill it together; the right structure falls out as you go.

How to use

Work through the sections in order — each answer sets up the next. Where something is hard to fill or you don’t yet agree, that’s a real term to settle now — while it’s cheap. A blank field is information, not a failure. Nothing here is a test of trust; it’s the structure both sides will rely on. Your answers save automatically in this browser.

New to the jargon? Plain-English glossary — vesting, SAFE, waterfall & more
Lens 01

Proportion

Reward tracks what each side brings — and follows the lasting value, not just the hours.

Lens 02

Clarity

Everything is named, priced, and agreed up front — clear to both, not assumed.

Lens 03

Continuity

If the deal ends or changes, each side keeps something fair and can carry on.

The Canvas
1
Goals, alternatives & alignmenti Start here — your real goal frames everything below. It’s what you want out of this, and it’s often different from the other side’s (that’s fine — write both, then design the deal so each is met). Your BATNA is your walk-away; the deal should beat it for both. The “I win when” lines and the goals should be compatible; if they conflict, adjust the structure, not the trust.
Proportion

Name what each side truly wants out of this (often different — that’s good), the best alternative if there’s no deal, and what winning looks like. Compatible-but-different goals are the sign of a great deal — you can design terms that serve both.

2
The businessi Define just enough of the business to make the deal fair — especially the channel and the edge, which are the durable moat (they feed sections 4 & 8). A party’s own business that buys is a customer at market terms, like any other.
Clarity

Who it’s for, what they buy, how they’re reached, and why it wins.

Strengthen the offer — the value equation
Make it easy to say yes (Hormozi’s value equation): value = (dream outcome × perceived likelihood) ÷ (time delay × effort) Raise the top, lower the bottom. Then stack: a bonus (solve the next objection), a guarantee (reverse the risk), and real scarcity / urgency (a genuine limit, never invented). Name it so the promise is unmistakable.
What good looks likei North Star: the one number that proves this is genuinely working — a value metric, not a vanity one (paying customers / MRR / engaged subscribers, never follower counts). e.g. a services business → MRR; a teaching / audience business → engaged email subscribers; this deal → external (non-partner) paying customers; a marketplace → transactions a week.
Two questions a handshake skipsi Both real-world deals this canvas was tested on hid the same two issues. Tick either to reveal what to do — both surfaced repeatedly and quietly subsidise one side when left unsaid.
Use two separate contracts so neither role absorbs the other. Watch work-for-hire: IP you build as an employee defaults to the employer unless the deal says otherwise.
Price it at an internal / transfer rate so it runs through the waterfall (section 6). Free-by-default hides a subsidy to one side.
3
Why this is a partnershipi Match each contribution to the right instrument: Pre-existing asset (you already own it) → license it to the venture or have it valued; it stays yours (section 8). Don’t fold it in as “co-building.” e.g. “the system I already built,” “my customer list.” One-off task → a fee. e.g. “build the website,” “write the launch copy.” Ongoing role → a salary / retainer. e.g. “run ops ~10h/week.” Co-building the uncertain → a share. e.g. “we’re both inventing the product, finding the first customers, and carrying the risk — no one knows yet if it sells.”
Proportion

What does each side bring that the other would otherwise have to buy or build? Tag each as a pre-existing asset, a task, a role, or co-building the uncertain — each is paid differently, and only co-building is what a share is for. A contribution can be more than one at once — e.g. an audience is a pre-existing asset (licensed in) and ongoing co-building (a share). Tag every part that applies, and pay each part its own way.

4
Contributions: durability & leverage
Proportion

Tag each contribution on two axes — durability (how long its value lasts → sets the lasting share in section 5) and leverage (how much it scales → what’s worth growing). They’re often different, and that gap is the point. Ownership on a breakup is settled separately, in section 8.

Durability & leverage — how to tag a contribution
Two different questions about each contribution. Don’t conflate them. Durability — how long the value lasts / how hard to copy (the moat). Sets who keeps a lasting share. Most durable first: distribution › brand & relationships › proprietary data › systems & judgment › code / content › capital › labour AI pushes code & generic content down (near-free to produce) and distribution, data & judgment up. Leverage — how much it scales without more input (Naval’s ladder). Tells you what to grow, not who to reward. labour (1:1) ‹ capital ‹ code ‹ media (scales while you sleep) The takeaway: reward the durable end; grow the high-leverage end — they’re usually different things. Software is the classic case: high leverage, low durability → build the business around it, but the lasting share goes to whoever owns the distribution, data, and brand. Worked example: “a system I built” → durability: systems & judgment; leverage: high.   “a list of 2,000 buyers” → durability: distribution (top); leverage: medium.   “running support” → durability: labour; leverage: low.
5
Setting the spliti A common default: contributions are paid at market rate through section 6; a share on top is earned over time. Reverse vesting (typical: 4 years, 1-year cliff): you each receive your shares/units now, but the company can buy back the unearned part if someone leaves early — so generosity on day one is safe.
Proportion

Peg numbers to objective standards (market rate, hours, capital at risk). Reward the durable end (section 4). Pick the vehicle, then set who holds what and how a share is earned.

Boxes 5, 6 and 8 lock together — a fair % often depends on paying a durable asset as a fee in the waterfall (6) and on who owns what (8). Expect to jog between them rather than settle 5 first.
Multi-stream deal? Split by revenue stream
Some deals don’t have one fair number — different revenue streams fairly carry different splits (e.g. software vs. services, delivery vs. sourcing). Optional: leave it untouched for a single-number deal; fill the rows that apply.
6
How money flows
Clarity

Money is paid in this order, each line with its reason:

  1. Direct costs of doing the work
  2. Tax set aside
  3. Market-rate pay for each contribution (from section 4)
  4. Remaining profit, split per section 5
7
Who decides whati “Both sign” marks a reserved matter — a decision big enough that neither side makes it alone. The rows below are a starting list; edit, add, or remove.
Clarity

One accountable owner per area. Tick the decisions big enough to need both signatures.

8
What each ownsi Brought in = pre-existing IP, brand, customers, data, tools — stays its owner’s, licensed to the venture. Made together = created during the deal — owned by the venture, with a licence back so each can keep working.
Continuity

Separate what each side brought in from what you make together. A right to use is not ownership.

9
How it can end or change
Continuity

Settle the ending while it’s cheap — written before entry.

10
Premortem — imagine it failedi Premortem (Gary Klein): picture it’s a year on and the deal went badly, then explain why. Imagining a certain failure surfaces risks that “what could go wrong?” never does — optimism hides them.
Continuity

It’s twelve months from now and this went badly. Write the story of why — then turn each cause into something to watch for or prevent.

Reading the Canvas · Three States
State 01

Keep talking

Fields are blank or you don’t yet agree. These are the real terms — settle them before anything is signed.

→ Work the open fields together.

State 02

Pilot first

The shape is clear but unproven — demand, fit, or how you work together. Test it on narrow, time-boxed terms.

→ Sign a short pilot memo, then revisit.

State 03

Ready to formalize

Every field is filled and agreed, pegged to objective standards, with endings written before entry.

→ Convert to the Agreement Memo, then a lawyer.
