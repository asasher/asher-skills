# The method

Depth behind the five moves in `SKILL.md`. Read at the start of a dissolution and whenever a move stalls.

## The core idea

A confused question is a fact about the **questioner**, not the world. "When you are confused, the confusion is
in your map, not in the territory." So you don't hunt for the answer out there — you trace, inside the mind, the
algorithm that *produces the sensation of an open question*. Do that well and the question doesn't get answered;
it stops being asked. The test is phenomenological: **if any feeling of a hanging question remains, you have not
dissolved it** — no matter how clever the analysis looks on the page.

Two failure shapes to avoid, both of which *feel* like progress:

- **Answering.** Picking a side and defending it. This leaves the itch intact — the other side still sounds
  arguable. If you're marshalling arguments *for* a position, you've stopped dissolving.
- **Declaring it meaningless.** "That's just semantics" is not a dissolution. You still owe the *stack trace*:
  why did a mind reliably generate this non-question and experience it as urgent? Proving a question empty
  without explaining the itch leaves the itch.

## Move 1 — the itch

Restating the question as a fact ("well, free will is just...") skips the work. Name the *sensation*: what makes
it feel unresolved? Usually one of — two intuitions that collide, a word that seems to point at something but you
can't say what, or a yes/no that seems to carry huge consequences with no way to check. That sensation is the
target. Everything downstream is trying to make it go away honestly.

## Move 2 — taboo (the workhorse)

**Taboo your words**: forbid the loaded term *and its synonyms* and force the sentence to be rebuilt out of
concrete substance. "Can I choose otherwise?" → taboo *choose*, *free* → "Does my decision depend on my
reasoning, or is it fixed regardless of what I think?" — a question with actual structure.

Taboo does two jobs at once:

- Where the rewrite **flows**, the word was a harmless label; keep the rewrite and move on.
- Where you **cannot** rewrite without sneaking the word back in, you have *found the confusion's address*. That
  irreplaceable spot is exactly what moves 3–4 dismantle.

Watch for weasel words that hide the whole problem: *real, true, actually, really, just, essentially, is.* "But
is it *really* a disease?" — the word *really* is where the confusion hides. Taboo it first.

## Move 3 — stack trace & unbundle

Two things happen here.

**The stack trace.** Write, step by step, how a mind arrives at the feeling of this question. Example (free
will): I model the world as deterministic → I also run a planner that represents my choices as *open* because I
can't see my own decision being computed → the two models collide → the collision is *experienced* as "so are my
choices free or not?" The question dissolves once you see it's an artifact of running two incompatible
self-models, not a fact awaiting discovery.

**The unbundling.** A single contested word usually packs several independent questions. Scott Alexander on
"disease": asking *"is alcoholism really a disease?"* is really asking a bundle —

1. Is it **bad** / harmful? (empirical-ish)
2. Is the person **at fault** — would blame or willpower change it? (empirical + moral)
3. Does treating it as **medical** (drugs, doctors, no blame) produce better outcomes than treating it as a
   character flaw? (empirical)
4. Do we owe the person **sympathy**? (moral)

Each has its own answer; the word "disease" only felt hard because it forced one yes/no onto four separate axes.
Diagram the split (Mermaid tree) so the human sees the one question become many.

**Watch the strand that feels too obvious to ask.** The trap in this bundle is strand 1 — *is it even bad?*
Because harm feels definitional, it gets smuggled in as a premise ("drinks *despite harm*") and never tested, so
the split comes out incomplete. Rule: a sub-question you have stated anywhere as a background fact is not yet
separated — pull it out and give it its own row in move 4. Every strand gets tested, especially the one you were
about to assume.

## Move 4 — anticipated experience

For each sub-question, ask: *what do I expect to observe if the answer is yes, versus no?* This is the sharpest
filter:

- If the two answers predict **different** experiences → the sub-question is **real**. Label it *empirical* (a
  fact to look up or test) or *value-laden* (a preference/ethics call, not a fact). Both are legitimate; naming
  which stops them from masquerading as each other.
- If the two answers predict the **same** experience → the sub-question is **empty**. It was pure word-attachment
  ("but is a virus *truly alive*?" changes no anticipation). Mark it dissolved-on-contact.

This is where most of the original question evaporates: typically one or two sub-questions are real and the rest
are empty.

## Move 5 — resolve & gate

Assemble what survived. A good dissolution usually ends in one of:

- **The question was empty** — every part predicted the same experience; the word was doing all the work.
- **The question was a bundle** — replaced by 2–3 sharper questions, each now answerable on its own terms
  (and some already answered in move 4).
- **A real disagreement, relocated** — the remaining dispute is a clearly-named value difference, no longer
  disguised as a factual one.

**Settling a relocated value question.** Naming it isn't the end. When the surviving strand is about how to
*treat* the case — assign blame, medicalize, extend sympathy — don't reach for a hidden fact (the person's
"true" degree of control) to settle it. Settle it by **consequences: which stance demonstrably produces better
outcomes?** Blame is set by what *helps*, not by what's deserved. Framed that way the strand is *a decision, not
a discovery* — showing that is itself the dissolution, not a verdict you impose. And once the parts are answered,
"but is it *really* a disease?" has no residue left; stop asking it.

Then the **gate**, asked *to the human*: "Read the original question again. Any lingering sense that something's
still unanswered?" If yes — good, that pull points at a word not yet tabooed or a sub-question not yet split.
Log where it is and loop back to move 2. Dissolution is done only when the human reads the original and feels the
question has *stopped being a question*.
