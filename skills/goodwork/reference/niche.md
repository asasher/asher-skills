# niche — candidate-market fit and where it lives

Position the person at the intersection of what they want, what the market wants, and how the market sees them (Never Search Alone's candidate-market fit), sharpened into a hard-to-copy intersection (Perell's personal monopoly), and located in real communities. Requires the profile's strengths, market position, and constraints sections.

## Step 1 — Draft the intersection

From the profile, generate two or three candidate niches of the form: **[strength] × [domain they've lived] × [audience/beneficiary] × [format]** — e.g., "data engineering × healthcare claims × mid-size payers × contracting," or "explaining complex systems × immigration law × founder-immigrants × writing + advisory."

Good niche criteria: complementary (the parts reinforce), useful (someone pays for it), specific (feels too narrow offline, viable online/at market scale), experiential (they can say "I've lived this"). Test each against:
- "Where do people already ask for your take?"
- "What can you explain unusually well because you lived it?"
- "What tiny audience would immediately care?"

## Step 2 — Reality-test as hypotheses, not identity

Each niche is a candidate-market-fit **hypothesis** written as: target roles/titles the market actually uses, the evidence you'd show, the likely objection, and what would falsify it. These get tested in the listening tour (`outreach`) — market conversations, not introspection, decide. Update after every ~5 conversations.

Two-pager discipline: condense to a two-page memo — situation, target hypotheses, constraints, evidence, open questions, specific asks. This is the artifact to share with mentors, peers, or a job-search council so they can challenge assumptions. If the user has 2–3 peers in the same boat, suggest forming one: weekly check-in, shared commitments — search morale and accountability are load-bearing.

## Step 3 — Map the communities

For the leading niche, build a community map — because referrals and weak ties, not portals, carry most opportunity:
- Search patterns: `[role] Slack`, `[tool] Discord`, `[industry] community`, `[city] [role] meetup`, `[conference] speakers`, `[project] good first issue`, plus newsletters, subreddits, guilds, alumni groups.
- Verify each is alive (recent activity, real practitioners, a jobs/gigs channel).
- Pick **3–5** to actually join. Participation cadence: lurk 3–7 days to learn norms; answer 2–3 questions a week; share one genuinely useful artifact; ask for advice before ever asking for referrals. Contribution before extraction — communities smell transactional joiners.

## Step 4 — Decide the visibility strategy

Match ambition to the niche: for internet-native/knowledge work, a proof-of-work cadence (one public artifact per week or two — handled in `assets`) compounds luck surface area (doing × telling). For fields where public building is unusual, visibility = speaking at the meetup, contributing to the guild, being known by the twenty people who matter. Don't prescribe an influencer path to someone whose market is twenty hiring managers.

## Output

Write `goodwork/NICHE.md`: the intersection hypotheses with falsifiers, the two-pager, the community map with join/participation plan, and the visibility strategy. Hand the hypotheses to `outreach` for the listening tour and the proof-of-work plan to `assets`.
