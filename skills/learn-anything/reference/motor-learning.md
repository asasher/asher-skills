# Motor learning — the science the coach hat runs on

Spaced retrieval and storage strength are *declarative*-memory science; they cover the teacher hat. Physical
skill runs on the motor-learning literature, which sometimes points the opposite way. These are the findings
sturdy enough to build protocol on.

## Stages (Fitts & Posner)

**Cognitive → associative → autonomous.** The stage sets the coaching mode; track it per drill.

- *Cognitive:* the learner is figuring out what the movement even is. Dense feedback, demonstrations,
  lessons, slow reps. Errors are large and instructive.
- *Associative:* the pattern exists; refinement is the work. This is where drills, interleaving, and
  calibrated gap measures live — most of a workspace's life is here.
- *Autonomous:* the skill runs without attention. Feedback thins to sampling; variability and pressure
  (performance contexts, human checkpoints) become the progression. Beware **overlearning past criterion**
  in unvaried conditions — it buys robustness cheaply at first, then plateaus; vary instead.

## Deliberate practice (Ericsson)

Practice counts when it has: a well-defined task, difficulty just past current ability, immediate feedback,
and repetition with correction. Hours without those properties correlate with *time spent*, not skill. The
five-part loop spec is this list operationalized. The under-quoted half: experts build **mental
representations** of the target — hence exemplar study and perception-before-production are practice, not
prelude.

## Contextual interference (interleaving)

Mixed, semi-random drill order feels worse and *performs* worse in the session than blocked repetition — and
retains and transfers far better. The learner's felt sense of fluency is anti-correlated with learning here,
which is why the agent, not the learner, runs the switching, and why the skill warns that practice should
feel effortful when it's working. Exception: at the cognitive stage, block first — interference helps only
once a rough pattern exists.

## The guidance hypothesis (faded feedback)

Constant feedback becomes a crutch: performance rides the feedback and collapses without it. Retention
improves when feedback is **thinned over time** — every rep at first, then sampled, then summary-only, then
learner-first ("you score it, then I'll tell you"). Withdrawing is a coaching act, not neglect. The endgame
of every loop is the learner carrying the gap measure in their own perception.

## Variable practice

Vary the parameters around the target — tempo, key, phrase, word-position, emotional register, floor surface
— rather than drilling one frozen rep. Variation builds the *schema*, not the instance, and is the main
defense against skills that work in the practice room and nowhere else.

## Attentional focus (Wulf)

External-focus cues (the effect in the world: the click, the back wall, the floor) reliably beat
internal-focus cues (the body part) for both performance and retention, across skill levels and domains.
This is one of the most replicated results in the field — write cues external-first, always.

## Part–whole and chunking

Decompose, automate the part, then **reassemble deliberately** — reassembly is its own drill, not a given.
Prefer natural joints (a phrase, a step-pattern, a phoneme-in-context) over arbitrary slices. Slowing down is
the universal part-practice: slow enough to be correct, because reps groove whatever is actually performed —
speed is a progression axis, never a starting point.

## Consolidation and spacing

Motor gains land substantially **after** the session, sleep-dependent: expect today's drill to be better in
two days and judge progress on next-session-first-reps, not end-of-session bests. Motor skills also decay
slower than facts — schedule on the drill's own score history, not on flashcard intervals
(`reference/scheduling.md`). Short frequent sessions beat rare marathons; fatigue past the stop condition
trains the error.

## Mental practice

Vivid imagery of correct execution measurably helps motor learning — a fraction of physical practice's
effect, but available on the bus. Legitimate as a supplement for autonomous-stage drills and as a warm-up;
never a substitute while the movement is still being found.
