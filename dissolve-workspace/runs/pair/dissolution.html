A Dissolution

Is alcoholism a disease?

1

The question, as posed

empty

State it in the asker's own words. Then name the itch — what makes this feel like a real, hanging question rather than a settled fact. The itch is the thing we're dissolving; the confusion lives in the map, not the territory.

As posed: "Is alcoholism a disease?" — asked as if it had a yes/no answer we could look up, the way we can ask "is tuberculosis a disease?"

The itch. Two intuitions collide and neither yields. On one side: the heavy drinker's body visibly changes — tolerance climbs, withdrawal shakes the hands, cravings override stated intentions; a person who wants to stop keeps drinking. That reads as something happening to them, like an illness. On the other side: people do quit, respond to prices, ultimatums, a new job, a bad scare — the behavior bends to incentives the way a choice does, not the way a tumor does.

The word disease sits on the fault line, and it feels load-bearing: answer "yes" and the drinker seems owed treatment and released from blame; answer "no" and it looks like a vice to be scolded or punished. So a small classification decision appears to carry the whole weight of fault, control, and what-we-owe-them at once. The itch is that this feels like discovering a hidden fact about the drinker when — as the taboo will show — most of what rides on it is a decision about which box to file the phenomenon under. The confusion is in the map (our category), not the territory (the drinking).

2

Taboo the words

empty

Ban the loaded word (free will, disease, fair, conscious, really) and re-ask using only what you actually mean. Where the substitution flows, the word was cheap. Where you cannot substitute without smuggling the word back — that spot is the confusion, now located.

Banned: disease and its synonyms — illness, sickness, medical condition, disorder — plus the weasels real, really, actually, just, is. Re-asking without them forces the packed meanings apart:

Banned wordWhat it was standing in for
disease → has a bodily mechanismChronic heavy drinking is accompanied by identifiable physical/biological changes (neuroadaptation, tolerance, withdrawal, heritable predisposition). Rewrites cleanly — this is a checkable claim.
disease → involuntaryThe person "cannot" refrain — behavior is not modifiable by ordinary choice/incentive. Here the word fights back: "cannot" can mean "responds weakly to incentives" (checkable) or "could not have done otherwise" (a metaphysical residue that predicts nothing). Confusion located.
disease → not their faultThe drinker is not morally blameworthy for the drinking or its harms. Cannot rewrite as a fact — this is a value assignment riding on the word. Confusion located.
disease → a medical matterThe fitting response is clinical treatment (not punishment, not willpower lectures). Rewrites into a what-works / what-we-should-do question.
disease → a natural kind"Alcoholism" names a discrete condition with a characteristic cause and course — not a shifting label we lay over one end of a drinking continuum. Rewrites into a checkable-but-partly-conceptual claim.
disease → the sick roleSociety should exempt the person from certain responsibilities and grant access to care/sympathy. A decision about entitlements, not a fact.

The word was cheap for "bodily mechanism" and "medical matter" — those re-ask fine. It was irreplaceable at two spots: involuntary (where "cannot" hides an untestable "could-not-have-done-otherwise") and not their fault (a value smuggled in as if it were a diagnosis). That is where the itch actually lives.

3

Stack trace & sub-questions

empty

Write the trace of the mental algorithm that generates the feeling of a question — not an answer to it. Then unbundle: a disguised question is usually several separate questions wearing one word. Diagram the decomposition.

The stack trace — how a mind manufactures the feeling of this question:

  1. Observe someone drinking heavily and being harmed by it.
  2. Notice they keep going after saying they want to stop → infer loss of control.
  3. Loss of control needs a category that explains it and lifts blame. The nearest ready-made box is disease (diabetes, cancer — things that befall you and aren't your fault).
  4. File the drinking under "disease." This simultaneously discharges three separate debts in one move: it posits a bodily cause, it excuses, and it prescribes treatment.
  5. But then notice the drinker does respond to incentives, sometimes quits cold → the "loss of control" premise wobbles → the box no longer fits snugly.
  6. Feeling the misfit, the mind asks "but is it really a disease?" — hoping a single yes/no will re-settle cause, blame, and treatment together. It won't, because step 4 bundled things that can come apart.

The premise I nearly smuggled. My own pose wrote "keeps drinking despite wanting to stop." That "despite" quietly asserts loss of control (sub-question C) as a background fact. It is not a premise — it is one of the things in dispute, so it gets its own yes/no below.

Unbundling. The one word packs at least six independent questions; answering one tells you little about the others:

graph TD
  Q["Is alcoholism a disease?"] --> B["B. bodily mechanism?"]
  Q --> C["C. involuntary / could-not-do-otherwise?"]
  Q --> K["K. natural kind or continuum?"]
  Q --> M["M. blameworthy?"]
  Q --> R["R. which response helps most?"]
  Q --> S["S. grant the sick role?"]
  C -.->|"'despite' smuggles this in"| M
  B -.->|"mechanism tempts a yes"| K
  M -.->|"answer shapes"| S
  R -.->|"answer shapes"| S

B and K are broadly empirical. C is the danger node — empirical if read as "how modifiable by incentives," empty if read as untestable metaphysics. M and S are value/decision questions. R is empirical about what works, but choosing by it is a decision. The next move (Anticipate) tests each strand against expected experience — left for CODEX and me to fill together.
