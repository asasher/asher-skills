A Dissolution

Is alcoholism a disease?

1

The question, as posed

empty

State it in the asker's own words. Then name the itch — what makes this feel like a real, hanging question rather than a settled fact. The itch is the thing we're dissolving; the confusion lives in the map, not the territory.

As posed: "Is alcoholism a disease?" — a flat yes/no that seems to ask which kind of thing compulsive drinking really is.

The itch. It feels like a hanging question because two intuitions collide over one word. Intuition A: the heavy drinker looks ill — their brain and body are visibly altered, it runs in families, it kills them, and they seem unable to stop even as it destroys their life. Intuition B: they choose to lift the glass, they sometimes quit, they respond to threats and rewards — that looks like a behaviour, a vice, a failure of will, not like cancer. The word "disease" seems to point at a fact in the world that would settle which intuition wins.

And the yes/no feels urgent because a whole verdict seems to ride on it: if it is a disease, then it is not the drinker's fault, they deserve treatment and compassion and coverage, not punishment; if it is not, then it is a choice and blame applies. So the question presents itself as a small factual switch that discharges a large moral and practical payload. That sensation — one bit of fact that seems to decide fault, funding, and self-image — is the itch. It lives in the map: in how a mind files things, not in the drinker's neurons.

2

Taboo the words

empty

Ban the loaded word (free will, disease, fair, conscious, really) and re-ask using only what you actually mean. Where the substitution flows, the word was cheap. Where you cannot substitute without smuggling the word back — that spot is the confusion, now located.

Ban disease — and the weasel words real / really / is / actually. Ask what "disease" is doing in the sentence. It turns out to pack at least six independent contents; I re-ask with each one made explicit.

Banned wordWhat it was standing in for
disease → biological substrateDoes chronic heavy drinking involve measurable brain/body changes — tolerance, withdrawal, altered reward circuitry, heritable risk? Rewrites cleanly. Cheap word here.
disease → involuntarinessCan the person stop at will, like flipping a switch, or is their control impaired / graded? Rewrites cleanly.
disease → natural course + treatabilityDoes it follow a characteristic trajectory and respond to medical/therapeutic intervention? Rewrites cleanly.
disease → exemption from blameShould the drinker not be held at fault for the drinking and its harms? Here the rewrite stops being about the drinker and becomes a stance we take. The word will not reduce to a fact.
disease → warrant for the sick roleShould society respond with care, coverage, and treatment rather than censure and punishment? A decision, not a description. Irreplaceable without importing an ought.
disease → a natural kind ("really one")Is there a fact of the matter — a joint in nature — that files this condition into the illness bin independent of our purposes? When I try to rewrite this into pure observation, nothing is left to observe; the word points at a filing decision, not a thing.

Located. The first three strands rewrite without loss — "disease" was a cheap shorthand for empirical facts. The last three cannot be rewritten without either dropping a moral exemption or smuggling in a decision about how to treat people. That is where the confusion sits: disease is a single word bridging an is (biology, control, course) and an ought (blame, the sick role), disguised as one factual category.

3

Stack trace & sub-questions

empty

Write the trace of the mental algorithm that generates the feeling of a question — not an answer to it. Then unbundle: a disguised question is usually several separate questions wearing one word. Diagram the decomposition.

Stack trace — how the mind manufactures the feeling of a question:

  1. The mind observes: some people drink compulsively, damaging themselves, and seem unable to stop.
  2. It reaches for a category to file the case under. Two folders are on hand: illness (like diabetes) and vice / weakness of will (like sloth or gluttony).
  3. Each folder arrives pre-loaded with moral defaults: illness → blameless, deserves care; vice → blameworthy, deserves discipline.
  4. "Disease" is simply the label on the illness folder. So "is it a disease?" gets felt as "which folder has reality already dropped this into?" — as if the universe pre-sorted it.
  5. But the two folders differ on several independent axes at once — biological substrate, voluntariness, blame, medical treatment — that need not move together.
  6. Alcoholism scores illness-like on some axes (brain changes, familial risk, loss of control) and vice-like on others (it involves choice, it responds to incentives, it can remit). The single folder-bit will not resolve.
  7. The mind, trying to read one bit off a thing that carries several, feels a stuck switch. That stuck switch is the "hanging question." No fact is missing; the question is mis-shaped.

Unbundle. The strand I kept assuming as background — "unable to stop", "loss of control" — is really its own contested yes/no (SQ2); I refuse to pass it as a premise. Split into six:

4

Reduce to anticipated experience

empty

Turn each sub-question into what you'd actually expect to observe if it went one way versus the other. A sub-question whose two answers predict the same experience is empty — mark it dissolved-on-contact. The rest are real and may need evidence.

Sub-questionIf yes, I'd anticipate…If no, I'd anticipate…Verdict
SQ1 Measurable biological changes? Tolerance, withdrawal syndromes, altered reward-circuit activity on imaging, elevated risk clustering in families. Heavy drinkers physiologically indistinguishable from abstainers; no withdrawal, no familial pattern. empirical — predictions differ; observation favours yes.
SQ2 Control impaired vs. free to stop at will? Continued drinking despite crushing incentives to quit, reported craving, relapse, distress on cessation — and partial responsiveness (some incentives, e.g. contingency management, do move behaviour). Instant, permanent, distress-free stopping the moment a sufficient reason appears (jail, job loss). empirical — differ; observation shows control is impaired but graded, not a clean switch. This gradedness is exactly why the binary jams.
SQ3 Characteristic course + treatment response? A recognisable trajectory; treatment measurably shifts outcomes vs. no treatment. Random walk; therapeutic intervention inert. empirical — differ; observation favours yes.
SQ4 Is the drinker to be blamed? Nothing about the person or their neurons is observably different whether I judge "blameworthy" or "blameless". Still nothing observably different — blame is a stance I adopt, not a property I detect. value — same anticipated experience either way; the descriptive layer is empty. It is a stance, and it leans on the deeper could-have-done-otherwise question.
SQ5 Extend care/sick-role vs. punish? Medical framing, funding, treatment access; plausibly higher recovery, lower stigma and downstream social cost. Censure and punishment; possible deterrence, but also plausibly worse recovery and higher net harm. value / decision — these do predict different downstream outcomes, so it is a real choice, settled by which response reduces harm — not by the metaphysics of "disease".
SQ6 Is "disease" a natural kind? A clean test sorting every condition into disease/not-disease, no borderline cases, no dependence on human purpose. Exactly the borderline disputes we see (obesity? addiction? aging? grief?), with definitions shifting to fit medical, insurance, and moral agendas. empty — the two answers collapse into the world we already observe (the second one). "Disease" marks a purpose-relative filing decision, not a joint in nature. Dissolved on contact.
5

Resolution

empty

State the dissolution: with the word tabooed and the sub-questions separated, the original question is answered, empty, or replaced by sharper ones. Then run the gate — is there any lingering feeling of a hanging question? If yes, it is not dissolved; note where the pull remains and keep going.

"Is alcoholism a disease?" is not one question but a bundle. Tabooed and split, it becomes three empirical questions — biological changes (yes), voluntary control (impaired but graded), course and treatability (yes) — plus one genuine value/policy decision (blame and the sick role, to be settled by which response reduces harm), all wrapped in a pseudo-factual "which natural kind did the universe file it under?" that is simply empty. There is no further fact "is it really a disease" hovering above the sub-answers, because "disease" is not a joint in nature — it is a classifying decision we make for a purpose.

Why the mind felt a hanging question: "disease" is the label on an illness folder that bundles biology + involuntariness + blamelessness + medical treatment into a single bit, sitting opposite a vice folder that bundles the opposites. Alcoholism splits the bundle — high on biology and impaired control, but retaining a residue of choice and incentive-responsiveness — so the one folder-bit will not latch. The stuck switch is the itch. Once the axes are pulled apart, each has its own clean answer and the switch dissolves.

Lingering-confusion check
Honest report: the itch specific to the word "disease" is gone — no strand is still fused under it, and I can see exactly why a mind generates the question and feels it as urgent. But a faint pull remains one layer down. SQ4's blame axis rests on "could the drinker have done otherwise?", and my empirical answer to SQ2 ("impaired but graded") does not draw the bright line that the moral intuition wants. That pull is not about "disease" — it is the free-will / could-have-done-otherwise question, correctly relocated beneath SQ4 rather than smuggled through the word "disease". So I judge this question dissolved, with that deeper question named and parked, not resolved. Calling the whole free-will problem solved here would be dishonest; leaving "disease" fused would also be dishonest. It is unbundled: the disease-confusion discharges cleanly, handing its remaining charge to a different, named question.

Open threads

  • Free will under SQ4. "Could the drinker have done otherwise?" — the bright line the blame intuition demands. Its own dissolution project; SQ4 borrows its charge from here.
  • SQ5 is an empirical bet dressed as ethics. "Which response reduces harm?" needs actual outcome data (recovery, deterrence, cost) that I have not gathered blind — the direction of the decision is contingent on evidence, not settled a priori.
  • Where to set the graded-control threshold. Since SQ2 is a continuum, any place we draw "impaired enough to excuse" is itself a decision, inheriting the SQ5 and free-will threads.
