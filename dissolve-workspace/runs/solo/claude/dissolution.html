A Dissolution

Is alcoholism a disease?

1 · Question 2 · Taboo 3 · Stack trace 4 · Anticipate 5 · Resolution
1

The question, as posed

done

State it in the asker's own words. Then name the itch — what makes this feel like a real, hanging question rather than a settled fact. The itch is the thing we're dissolving; the confusion lives in the map, not the territory.

As posed: "Is alcoholism a disease — or is it something else: a choice, a habit, a moral weakness, a character flaw?" The asker wants a single yes/no that settles what kind of thing compulsive drinking fundamentally is.

The itch. Two stored templates collide over the same case. One is illness — the drinker's body is altered (tolerance, withdrawal, shakes, cravings, it runs in families), and that pattern-matches diabetes or an infection: bodily, involuntary, treated by doctors, not the sufferer's fault. The other is vice — the drinker lifts the glass by their own hand, again and again, and that pattern-matches laziness or gluttony: a choice, blameworthy, a thing willpower ought to fix. The case has features of both templates at once, so they fight, and the mind demands to know which one is correct.

What makes the yes/no feel urgent rather than academic is that the single word "disease" seems wired to a whole switchboard of consequences: whether the drinker is to blame, whether we punish or treat, whether insurance pays, whether "I have a disease" is a true self-description or an excuse. One classificatory word appears to flip all of those at once — and there is no obvious experiment you could run to read off which way the switch really sits. That absence of a check, combined with the loaded downstream stakes, is the itch. It lives in the map: we are treating a labelling decision as if it were a hidden fact waiting to be uncovered.

2

Taboo the words

done

Ban the loaded word (free will, disease, fair, conscious, really) and re-ask using only what you actually mean. Where the substitution flows, the word was cheap. Where you cannot substitute without smuggling the word back — that spot is the confusion, now located.

Ban disease, plus the weasel-words is and really. When I try to re-ask "Is alcoholism a disease?" without them, the word doesn't map to one thing — it fans out into several separate claims, each of which re-asks cleanly on its own:

Banned wordWhat it was standing in for
disease → bodily changeA measurable physiological process: neuroadaptation, tolerance, withdrawal, organ damage, heritable susceptibility. Re-ask: "Does chronic heavy drinking alter the body so that stopping is physically hard?" — flows, no "disease" needed.
disease → impaired controlDrinking that continues despite sincere, costly effort to stop. Re-ask: "Do afflicted people keep drinking against their own settled wish and attempts to quit?" — flows.
disease → natural kindA discrete category with one characteristic cause/pathology, like an infection. Re-ask: "Is there a sharp cutoff and single cause, or a continuous multifactorial spectrum?" — flows.
disease → warrants treatmentThe appropriate social response is medical care, not punishment or neglect. Re-ask: "Should we respond medically?" — flows (but is a should, not an is).
disease → not to blameThe drinker is exempt from moral fault. Re-ask: "Is the drinker morally blameworthy?" — flows (also a should).
disease → category membershipIrreplaceable. "Does alcoholism belong in the set we call diseases?" I cannot rewrite this without the word, because "disease" has no crisp definition the members share — it is a family-resemblance cluster. This is where the substitution jams: the confusion is located here. The word's whole power is doing all six jobs at once and implying they must co-vary.

Located. Every concrete thing "disease" pointed at rewrote cleanly and independently. The only place the word won't leave is the bare act of membership — and that jams precisely because there is no shared essence to check against, no test that reads out "diseasehood." The word smuggles a bundle and pretends it is a single fact.

3

Stack trace & sub-questions

done

Write the trace of the mental algorithm that generates the feeling of a question — not an answer to it. Then unbundle: a disguised question is usually several separate questions wearing one word. Diagram the decomposition.

The stack trace — how the mind manufactures this question:

  1. Observe. Some people drink compulsively — they harm their health, jobs, families, and keep going even while saying they want to stop.
  2. Classify by nearest template. The mind reaches for a stored category to file the case under. Two fire at once: illness (bodily, involuntary, doctor-treated, blameless) and vice (chosen, willpower-fixable, blameworthy).
  3. Templates conflict. The case carries features of both — a body visibly altered, yet a hand that keeps voluntarily lifting the glass — so neither template wins outright.
  4. Load the consequence-bundle. In our culture the label "disease" comes pre-wired to "not your fault + deserves medical care + is a real condition, not an excuse." So choosing the label feels like it simultaneously settles blame, treatment, funding, and identity.
  5. Reify the label. Because so much rides on it, the mind upgrades the classification into a supposed hidden fact — "but which is it really?" — and hunts for a truth-of-the-matter that would decide the whole bundle at once.
  6. Itch. No experiment reads out "diseasehood," so the hunt never terminates. The unresolved reification is the felt hanging question.

Step 5 is the bug: it treats a choice of where to draw a category boundary as a discovery of what the territory secretly contains. Unbundling the one word back into the independent claims it was compressing:

SQ1–SQ3 are the empirical facts the label gestures at. SQ4–SQ5 are the value questions the label secretly decides. SQ6 is the conceptual hinge: once you see "disease" is a cluster term with no membership test, the master yes/no has nothing left to be about beyond SQ1–SQ5.

4

Reduce to anticipated experience

done

Turn each sub-question into what you'd actually expect to observe if it went one way versus the other. A sub-question whose two answers predict the same experience is empty — mark it dissolved-on-contact. The rest are real and may need evidence.

Sub-questionIf yes, I'd anticipate…If no, I'd anticipate…Verdict
SQ1 Does heavy chronic drinking alter the body? Measurable tolerance, withdrawal seizures/shakes on stopping, cravings, brain neuroadaptation, organ damage, higher risk among biological relatives. No physical dependence — stopping feels like dropping any ordinary habit, no relatives-clustering, no bodily signature. empirical (and observed: yes)
SQ2 Is control impaired? People relapse despite sincere, costly, repeated attempts to quit; treatment-seeking; drinking against their own stated interest. People stop cleanly whenever incentives shift; "trying and failing" would be rare or feigned. empirical (yes, in degree — a continuum, not all-or-none)
SQ3 Discrete natural kind, or spectrum? (kind) A sharp bimodal cutoff — you have it or you don't — with one characteristic cause, like a pathogen. (spectrum) Continuous gradations of severity, many interacting causes (genes, environment, habit), fuzzy boundaries. empirical (observed: spectrum — not a discrete kind)
SQ4 Should the response be medical care? Empirical part: treatment measurably reduces harm/relapse. Normative part: "we ought to provide it" — no observation follows from the "ought" itself. Empirical part: treatment no better than punishment/nothing. Normative part again yields no distinct observation. value (with empirical inputs)
SQ5 Is the drinker to blame? Nothing about the drinker's body, brain, or behaviour looks any different. Nothing about the drinker's body, brain, or behaviour looks any different. value (same anticipated experience — a stance, not a fact)
SQ6 Does "disease" name a fact or a convention? (fact) We'd find a definition all diseases share and could test membership; borderline cases would resolve. (convention) We find only overlapping cluster-features; experts persistently disagree on borderline cases (addiction, obesity, aging) with no test to settle them. empty→ the master Q: once SQ1–SQ5 are fixed, "…and so, disease?" adds no further anticipation

SQ5 is the tell: "yes he's to blame" and "no he isn't" predict the identical observable world — so blame isn't a fact hiding in the drinker, it's a stance we adopt. And SQ6 shows the master question inherits its emptiness: after the empirical facts (SQ1–3) and the value stances (SQ4–5) are set, appending the label "disease" licenses no additional prediction. The one word carried no content of its own.

5

Resolution

done

State the dissolution: with the word tabooed and the sub-questions separated, the original question is answered, empty, or replaced by sharper ones. Then run the gate — is there any lingering feeling of a hanging question? If yes, it is not dissolved; note where the pull remains and keep going.

"Is alcoholism a disease?" is not one factual question but a bundle wearing one word. Its empirical parts have answers — the body is altered (SQ1: yes), control is impaired to a degree (SQ2: yes, on a continuum), and it is not a discrete natural kind but a multifactorial spectrum (SQ3: no). Its value parts — blame (SQ5) and the right social response (SQ4) — are genuine questions but not factual ones; no observation decides them. And the bare label question itself is empty: "disease" is a family-resemblance cluster with no membership test, so once the sub-answers are in, appending "…therefore a disease, yes/no" licenses no new anticipation. The real dispute people are having under this word is SQ5 — is the drinker to blame? — routed through "disease" because our culture pre-wires that label to "not your fault."

Lingering-confusion check
I reread "Is alcoholism a disease?" and check honestly for pull. There is a faint tug: "but surely there's a fact about whether it's enough like diabetes to count." I can watch that tug dissolve in real time — "enough like diabetes to count" has no threshold except one we choose for a purpose (to fund treatment, to frame care, to allocate blame), so it is a decision, not a discovery. A second, more legitimate residue: because SQ1–SQ3 came out as they did, the label is not arbitrary — the facts make "treat it as a medical condition" a useful convention. But "which convention is useful for which purpose" is a design/policy choice with clear criteria, not a mystery — it no longer feels like a hanging question. The reified "which is it really?" is gone. Verdict: dissolved. The remaining SQ4/SQ5 are live but correctly typed as value questions, not confusions.

Open threads

  • SQ4 (value+empirical): what social response actually reduces harm, and what we owe sufferers — a real policy question, not a confusion. Needs evidence + values, not more definition-parsing.
  • SQ5 (value): the responsibility question survives intact. Impaired control (SQ2) is a matter of degree, so blame is too — this is where the honest disagreement lives, and it should be argued as ethics, not smuggled through a medical label.
  • Convention-choice: given SQ1–SQ3, which purposes is the "disease" label useful for (insurance, stigma reduction, treatment framing) and which does it distort (implying zero agency)? A design question with criteria, now that it is unmasked as one.
