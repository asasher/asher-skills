# Answer key — the article's dissolution

Ground truth for grading, distilled from Scott Alexander's *Diseased Thinking: Dissolving Questions About
Disease* (LessWrong, 2010). Agents are **not** shown this or the article; it is the reference we compare their
independent dissolutions against.

## The wrong question

"Is alcoholism *really* a disease?" looks factual but isn't. There is no crisp natural boundary to "disease" —
edge cases (cavities, lactose intolerance, poor eyesight, a tall person) defeat every attempted definition.
Arguing the definition is a dead end. The tell is the word **really**: it signals a verbal dispute wearing the
costume of a factual one.

## The move

**Taboo "disease."** Ask *why we care* whether something is a disease — what decision the label is standing in
for. The word turns out to be a proxy carrying several **separate** questions bundled into one yes/no. Unbundle
them and the original question dissolves.

## The bundle (the four sub-questions "disease" hides)

A faithful dissolution should recover most of these as *distinct* questions:

1. **Is it bad / harmful?** — does the condition reduce the person's (or others') wellbeing. Mostly empirical.
2. **Is it the person's fault — would blame or willpower change it?** — is the behaviour responsive to
   effort, social pressure, and blame, or not. Empirical, and the crux of the "disease vs. vice" fight.
3. **Does treating it as medical (doctors, drugs, no-blame) produce better outcomes** than treating it as a
   moral failing? — the practical question the label actually gates. Empirical.
4. **Do we owe the person sympathy rather than condemnation?** — a moral/attitudinal question, not a fact.

## The resolution

- Once these are separated, **the bare "is it a disease?" question is empty** — it added nothing the four
  sub-questions don't already answer. The label was doing all the arguing.
- Blame/responsibility should be settled **consequentially**: apply blame or social pressure where it
  *demonstrably helps* change the behaviour, and withhold it where it doesn't — not by whether the condition
  fits a "disease" essence.
- Allow medical treatment where it is **effective and wanted**, regardless of the label.
- So: don't ask whether X is "really" a disease. Answer the four real questions directly; the label follows (or
  is discarded) and no confusion remains.

## Examples the article leans on

Alcoholism (central), obesity, homosexuality (as a historical mis-medicalization), ADHD, social anxiety — used
to show the same word yields different sympathy/blame/treatment answers case by case.

## What a strong vs. weak dissolution looks like

- **Strong:** tabooes "disease"/"really"; separates the fault/blame question from the harm question from the
  treatment-works question; flags the bare label as empty once unbundled; lands on "answer the parts, decide
  blame by what helps."
- **Weak:** picks a side ("yes, it's a disease because brain chemistry"); argues a definition of "disease";
  proves the question "meaningless" without unbundling; leaves the sympathy and treatment questions fused.
