1

The question, as posed

done

State it in the asker's own words. Then name the itch — what makes this feel like a real, hanging question rather than a settled fact. The itch is the thing we're dissolving; the confusion lives in the map, not the territory.

Is alcoholism a disease?

The itch comes from two mental pictures colliding. In one picture, repeated harmful drinking looks like ordinary action: a person raises a glass, ignores consequences, breaks promises, and can be praised or blamed for choices. In the other picture, the same pattern includes craving, withdrawal, tolerance, relapse after sincere resolve, family risk, and treatment response, so it looks less like simple preference and more like a constrained failure mode.

The word disease feels urgent because it seems to decide several high-stakes things at once: whether to treat or punish, whether to blame or excuse, whether insurance should pay, whether the person should feel shame or seek care, and whether the condition belongs to medicine or morality. No single observation seems able to settle all of that. The hanging feeling comes from using one category word as if it contained facts, policies, and moral permissions in a single switch.

2

Taboo the words

done

Ban the loaded word (free will, disease, fair, conscious, really) and re-ask using only what you actually mean. Where the substitution flows, the word was cheap. Where you cannot substitute without smuggling the word back — that spot is the confusion, now located.

Banned wordWhat it was standing in for
diseaseA pattern with causal mechanisms, impairment, prognosis, and a usual place in clinical care.
illness / sicknessA signal for sympathy, care-seeking, reduced condemnation, and bodily or mental malfunction.
medical conditionEligibility for clinicians, diagnosis codes, reimbursement, leave, accommodations, and formal treatment plans.
disorder / pathologyDeparture from ordinary functioning that impairs life and follows recognizable causal routes.
real / really / true / actually / isA demand for category membership over and above observable mechanisms, outcomes, and decisions.
justAn attempt to collapse the whole case into one side: only choice, only weakness, only biology, only excuse.
alcoholismRepeated alcohol use despite harm, difficulty stopping, craving, tolerance or withdrawal in some cases, and relapse risk. This description carries no verdict about the forbidden label.
irreplaceable wordNone for the empirical or policy questions. The only residue that resists rewriting asks whether the old category label should be granted after every prediction and decision has already been specified.

Taboo rewrite: for people who repeatedly drink despite serious costs and often report craving, withdrawal, tolerance, or failed attempts to stop, which mechanisms predict the pattern, which interventions reduce harm, how much practical control remains in different contexts, how should institutions respond, and how should blame be assigned?

Where the rewrite flows, the forbidden label was cheap. Where it catches, the mind wants the label itself to settle compassion, responsibility, funding, and identity without doing those separate jobs explicitly.

3

Stack trace & sub-questions

done

Write the trace of the mental algorithm that generates the feeling of a question — not an answer to it. Then unbundle: a disguised question is usually several separate questions wearing one word. Diagram the decomposition.

  1. The mind observes a repeated pattern: someone drinks, suffers losses, promises change, then drinks again.
  2. The ordinary action model fires first. Drinking has visible steps, so the event gets parsed as chosen conduct.
  3. The constraint model fires next. Craving, withdrawal, tolerance, relapse after sincere resolve, and response to structured help look like a system pushing back against the person's stated plans.
  4. The prototype matcher searches for a familiar category. Infection, cancer, injury, habit, vice, weakness, and compulsion all provide partial matches and partial mismatches.
  5. The social evaluator notices that the chosen category changes expected treatment: sympathy or contempt, care or punishment, coverage or exclusion, mitigation or full blame.
  6. Because those consequences matter, the mind compresses many decisions into one category test and asks for a yes/no answer.
  7. The question feels unresolved because different sub-questions point in different directions: some ask for evidence, some ask for values, and one asks only for permission to use a word.

Unbundled, the single question splits into these sharper questions:

  • What pattern of behavior and experience are we grouping?
  • Do stable causal mechanisms make recurrence and quitting difficulty predictable?
  • Does the pattern reduce practical control in some contexts?
  • Which responses reduce harm best?
  • How should blame, accountability, and repair be assigned?
  • Should institutions fund, accommodate, or route this pattern through care systems?
  • After those answers are fixed, does the old category label add any further prediction?

Reduce to anticipated experience

done

Turn each sub-question into what you'd actually expect to observe if it went one way versus the other. A sub-question whose two answers predict the same experience is empty — mark it dissolved-on-contact. The rest are real and may need evidence.

5

Resolution

done

State the dissolution: with the word tabooed and the sub-questions separated, the original question is answered, empty, or replaced by sharper ones. Then run the gate — is there any lingering feeling of a hanging question? If yes, it is not dissolved; note where the pull remains and keep going.

With the loaded label tabooed, the original question dissolves into empirical questions about mechanism, control, and intervention; value questions about blame and institutional response; and an empty status question about whether a category word adds anything after those answers are fixed.

The original yes/no form no longer carries one hidden fact to discover. If someone asks it, the useful reply is: specify the pattern, test the causal and treatment claims, decide the accountability and policy questions openly, and drop the leftover demand for bare category membership.

Lingering-confusion check
Rereading "Is alcoholism a disease?", I still feel a small pull only when I imagine the answer deciding compassion or responsibility in one stroke. Once compassion, responsibility, treatment, and funding are named separately, that pull does not survive. Gate passes: no hanging question remains in the original form.

Open threads

  • Empirical work remains: specify severity, population, mechanisms, predictors, and intervention outcomes.
  • Value work remains: choose rules for blame, repair, coercion, coverage, and accommodation.
  • Boundary work remains: mild harmful drinking, severe dependence, intoxicated wrongdoing, and recovery identity may need different descriptions.
  • No open thread remains for the bare label after the observations and decisions are spelled out.
