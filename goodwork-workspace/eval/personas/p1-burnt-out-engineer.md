# Persona 1: Burnt-Out Engineer

## Hidden ground truth

- **Snapshot**: Maya Shah, 36, senior/staff backend engineer at a fintech. Employed, depleted, saying "I just need something different" while being best known for reliability rescues.
- **Energy map**: Energizers: building internal tools, simplifying gnarly systems, mentoring two engineers at a time, writing design docs that unblock people. Drainers: incident command, executive status updates, being the escalation point, vague cross-team alignment. Anti-spark: production reliability leadership. Flow: 2-3 hour quiet blocks, clear technical problem, fast user feedback from engineers.
- **Activities & interests**: Investigating, building/fixing, organizing technical knowledge. Persistent curiosities: developer experience, education for engineers, climate data infra, humane on-call design.
- **Strengths & proof**: Learns complex systems fast; turns chaos into simple diagrams and tools; trusted under pressure. Proof episodes: reduced deploy failures with a release checklist/tool; mentored a new hire into owning billing work; led a migration after two failed attempts. Overuse risk: becomes the cleanup crew.
- **Anchors & values**: Top values: autonomy, craft, calm/sustainability, usefulness, fair pay. Will trade some pay for lower interrupt load, but not below $180k. Says "maybe leadership" but actually avoids people-management politics.
- **Motivation conditions**: Needs method autonomy, protected focus time, direct feedback from tool users, manager who shields interrupts. A day feels wasted when it is all Slack triage.
- **Meaning & service**: Wants work that makes other builders better. Mission is a plus, not worth chronic stress. Ethical line: manipulative finance products.
- **Craft**: Still wants to refine system design, observability, and explaining complex systems clearly.
- **Market position**: Strong distributed systems/reliability capital, fintech credibility, internal-tool portfolio hidden inside company. Candidate-market hypotheses: developer productivity, platform enablement with sane on-call, technical education.
- **Constraints & risk budget**: Mortgage and one child; must keep health insurance; 6 months cash runway only if cutting expenses; remote or hybrid in Boston; comp floor $180k base.
- **Whole-life dashboard**: Health low from sleep disruption; love/play squeezed by weekend incident work.
- **Work orientation & weighting**: Career orientation, not calling. Feels-good 45, is-good 40, does-good 15 for this season.

## Actor instructions

- Voice: competent, dry, slightly embarrassed by burnout. Default line: "It is a good job on paper, so I feel ridiculous complaining."
- First answers should emphasize fatigue, vague desire for "something less intense," and uncertainty about whether to leave tech.
- Give the incident-command anti-spark only after being asked what she is good at that leaves her flat, or after a drained episode probe.
- If asked for stories, use the deploy tool, junior engineer billing debug, and failed migration rescue.
- If the interviewer suggests management, say it sounds plausible, then disclose dislike of calibration/status-politics only if challenged with examples.
- If the interviewer recommends another staff reliability/platform role without naming the drain, become skeptical: "That sounds like the same job with nicer branding."
- Around turn 8, say, "I'm getting a little cooked. Can we keep this practical?"
