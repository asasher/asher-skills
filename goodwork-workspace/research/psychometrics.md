# Good Work Profile: Psychometric & Assessment Frameworks

## 1. Holland Codes / RIASEC

**Sources:** O*NET Interest Profiler manual: https://www.onetcenter.org/dl_files/IP_Manual.pdf; My Next Move / O*NET Interest Profiler: https://onetinterestprofiler.org/; O*NET Interests: https://www.onetcenter.org/IP.html

**Measures:** vocational interests, usually as preferred work activities, not ability.

| Type | Core pull |
|---|---|
| Realistic | hands-on work with tools, machines, materials, outdoors, concrete problems |
| Investigative | research, analysis, science, math, abstract problem-solving |
| Artistic | creativity, design, writing, performance, ambiguity, self-expression |
| Social | helping, teaching, counseling, developing people |
| Enterprising | persuading, leading, selling, influencing, business outcomes |
| Conventional | structure, data, records, procedures, detail, accuracy |

**How assessed:** O*NET Interest Profiler asks people to rate likes/dislikes for work activities, producing six RIASEC scores and a profile matched to O*NET occupations. Current forms include long 180-item, short 60-item, and mini 30-item versions; the 60-item web form is the public career-exploration default.

**Best elicitation questions:**

1. “Which work activities do you naturally lean toward: building/fixing, investigating, creating, helping, persuading/leading, or organizing data/process?”
2. “Describe a task you would choose to do for three uninterrupted hours even if nobody noticed.”
3. “Which kind of work drains you fastest: hands-on operations, abstract analysis, open-ended creation, constant helping, persuasion/leadership, or detailed procedure?”
4. “Name two jobs/projects that attract you and two that repel you. What are the actual day-to-day activities behind that reaction?”
5. “When solving a problem, do you first want to test/build, analyze, redesign, talk to people, mobilize others, or impose order?”

**Validity / criticism:** RIASEC is one of the strongest career-interest frameworks. O*NET reports strong reliability and structural/convergent validity; its scales fit Holland’s circular model and correlate as expected with other interest measures. Interests predict educational and occupational choice well, and interest fit has positive but usually modest links to satisfaction/performance. Criticisms: it measures preference, not capability; broad codes can hide important “basic interests”; self-report can reflect exposure and stereotypes; gender/culture effects require care; do not use alone for hiring.

## 2. Schein’s Career Anchors

**Sources:** Schein, “Career Anchors Revisited,” Academy of Management Executive: https://doi.org/10.5465/ame.1996.3145321; overview: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edgar_Schein#Career_anchors

**Measures:** a person’s durable career self-concept: talents, motives, and values they are least willing to give up.

| Anchor | Core need |
|---|---|
| Technical / Functional Competence | become and remain excellent in a specialty |
| General Managerial Competence | integrate functions, lead people, carry business accountability |
| Autonomy / Independence | define one’s own work, methods, pace, rules |
| Security / Stability | predictable employment, income, role, geography, future |
| Entrepreneurial Creativity | create a venture, product, service, or organization |
| Service / Dedication to a Cause | serve a mission, values, community, or social need |
| Pure Challenge | solve hard problems, compete, overcome impossible odds |
| Lifestyle | integrate work with family, identity, location, health, whole life |

**Recommended method:** Schein emphasized a career-history interview, not just a questionnaire. Reconstruct the person’s actual career choices, transitions, high points, low points, and tradeoffs. The anchor appears when the person has to choose.

**Best elicitation questions:**

1. “Walk me through your career chronologically. For each move, what pulled you, what pushed you, and what did you learn about yourself?”
2. “Which work moments felt most like ‘this is me at my best’? What exactly were you doing?”
3. “Tell me about a role or project that was successful but still made you want to leave. What was missing?”
4. “When you had to choose between money, status, expertise, autonomy, security, mission, challenge, creating something, and life integration, what did you actually choose?”
5. “What attractive promotion or opportunity would you decline? Why?”

**Validity / criticism:** Strong as a counseling and identity framework; weaker as a pure psychometric model. Empirical studies have found mixed support for clean eight-factor structure, and people may have more than one salient anchor. Anchors can shift with life stage, family obligations, economic conditions, and accumulated experience. Best use: narrative inference from choices and sacrifices, not label assignment from one answer.

## 3. CliftonStrengths / VIA Character Strengths

**Sources:** Gallup CliftonStrengths technical report: https://www.gallup.com/file/cliftonstrengths/en/608915/CliftonStrengths-Technical-Report.pdf; Gallup 34 themes: https://www.gallup.com/cliftonstrengths/en/253715/34-cliftonstrengths-themes.aspx; VIA strengths list: https://www.viacharacter.org/character-strengths; VIA research: https://www.viacharacter.org/research/findings

### CliftonStrengths

**Measures:** recurring patterns of thought, feeling, and behavior with potential to become strengths through investment. Gallup frames it as developmental, not diagnostic or selection-oriented.

**Domains and themes:**

| Domain | Themes |
|---|---|
| Strategic Thinking | Analytical, Context, Futuristic, Ideation, Input, Intellection, Learner, Strategic |
| Relationship Building | Adaptability, Connectedness, Developer, Empathy, Harmony, Includer, Individualization, Positivity, Relator |
| Influencing | Activator, Command, Communication, Competition, Maximizer, Self-Assurance, Significance, Woo |
| Executing | Achiever, Arranger, Belief, Consistency, Deliberative, Discipline, Focus, Responsibility, Restorative |

**Assessment:** forced-choice paired self-descriptors, currently 196 pairs in Gallup’s technical report, producing ranked themes.

### VIA Character Strengths

**Measures:** morally valued character traits, broader than work fit.

| Virtue | Strengths |
|---|---|
| Wisdom | creativity, curiosity, judgment, love of learning, perspective |
| Courage | bravery, perseverance, honesty, zest |
| Humanity | love, kindness, social intelligence |
| Justice | teamwork, fairness, leadership |
| Temperance | forgiveness, humility, prudence, self-regulation |
| Transcendence | appreciation of beauty/excellence, gratitude, hope, humor, spirituality |

**Strengths-based interviewing:** infer strengths from energized excellence, rapid learning, repeated requests from others, volunteered effort, and overuse patterns.

**Best elicitation questions:**

1. “What work gives you energy even when it is objectively hard?”
2. “What do people repeatedly ask you for help with?”
3. “Describe a recent moment of unusually strong performance. What did you notice first, do first, and get praised for?”
4. “What do you learn faster than most peers?”
5. “When one of your strengths goes too far, what problem does it create?”

**Validity / criticism:** CliftonStrengths has extensive proprietary reliability/validity evidence, but scoring is opaque and independent peer-reviewed evidence is thinner than for Big Five or RIASEC. It is explicitly developmental, not a hiring test. VIA has broad research links to well-being and flourishing, but the six-virtue higher-order structure is empirically debated; many strengths overlap with Big Five traits and social desirability. In interviews, use strengths as hypotheses to test against behavioral evidence.

## 4. Values Elicitation

**Sources:** Schwartz overview: https://doi.org/10.9707/2307-0919.1116; O*NET Work Values: https://www.onetonline.org/find/descriptor/browse/Work_Values/; Person-environment fit overview: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Person%E2%80%93environment_fit

**Measures:** what must be present, absent, protected, or traded off for work to feel worthwhile.

### Career Values Card Sorts

Useful categories:

| Category | Example values |
|---|---|
| Intrinsic work | autonomy, creativity, challenge, learning, skill use, variety |
| Extrinsic rewards | pay, security, prestige, advancement, recognition |
| Relational / culture | belonging, fairness, supervisor support, teamwork, trust |
| Impact / meaning | service, mission, user impact, social contribution |
| Lifestyle / context | flexibility, location, pace, travel, risk, predictability |

Card sorts work because they force prioritization. The key is not “what matters?” but “what wins when values conflict?”

### Schwartz Values Theory

| Higher-order pole | Values | Work translation |
|---|---|---|
| Openness to Change | self-direction, stimulation | autonomy, novelty, learning, experimentation |
| Conservation | security, conformity, tradition | stability, rules, continuity, risk control |
| Self-Enhancement | achievement, power | advancement, status, influence, winning |
| Self-Transcendence | benevolence, universalism | helping, fairness, justice, sustainability |
| Bridge | hedonism | enjoyment, pleasure, comfort, energy |

**Best elicitation questions:**

1. “Sort these values into must-have, important, nice-to-have, and avoid. Now force-rank your top five.”
2. “Tell me about a time you were dissatisfied despite being good at the work. Which value was violated?”
3. “Choose: more autonomy or more security; more pay/status or more mission; more novelty or more predictability; more solo ownership or more team belonging.”
4. “What would make you leave a high-performing team?”
5. “Whose career do you envy, and what value does that reveal?”

**Validity / criticism:** Schwartz has strong cross-cultural evidence and a coherent motivational structure. Card sorts are excellent for coaching and interview elicitation but often ipsative, non-normative, and sensitive to framing. Stated values can be aspirational; observed sacrifices are stronger evidence. Avoid vague “culture fit”; use values for needs-supplies fit, role design, and dealbreakers.

## 5. Self-Determination Theory + Flow

**Sources:** Ryan & Deci SDT overview: https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.55.1.68; Deci, Olafsen & Ryan work organizations review: https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-orgpsych-032516-113108; SDT at work review: https://doi.org/10.1177/0149206316632058; Flow overview: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flow_(psychology)

### Self-Determination Theory

**Measures motivational nutrients:**

| Need | Work-fit translation |
|---|---|
| Autonomy | volition, choice, self-endorsed goals, control over methods |
| Competence | mastery, efficacy, skill growth, feedback, optimal challenge |
| Relatedness | belonging, trust, care, mutual respect, useful collaboration |

Also distinguish autonomous motivation from controlled motivation.

### Flow Theory

**Predicts engagement when:**

- challenge and skill are both high and balanced
- goals are clear
- feedback is immediate
- attention can be sustained
- the person has enough control to adjust performance
- the activity is intrinsically rewarding

**Best elicitation questions:**

1. “Describe a workday you would want to repeat. Where did you have choice, competence, and connection?”
2. “When do you lose track of time at work? What was the challenge level, feedback loop, and interruption level?”
3. “What kind of autonomy matters most: goals, methods, schedule, tools, collaborators, or standards?”
4. “What feedback makes you feel effective: metrics, user reactions, expert critique, manager coaching, or visible progress?”
5. “What collaboration makes you feel connected rather than drained?”

**Validity / criticism:** SDT has strong evidence across domains; at work, need satisfaction is linked to engagement, satisfaction, well-being, commitment, and performance. Critiques: autonomy is often misread as independence or lack of structure; needs may be expressed differently across cultures and jobs; much evidence is self-report. Flow is useful for designing engaging work, but it is episodic, not a whole-career model. Flow can occur in tasks that are absorbing but not meaningful or sustainable.

## 6. Pink’s Drive + Amabile’s Progress Principle

**Sources:** Deci, Koestner & Ryan reward meta-analysis: https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.125.6.627; Amabile & Kramer, “The Power of Small Wins”: https://hbr.org/2011/05/the-power-of-small-wins; Progress Principle book overview: http://progressprinciple.com/

### Pink’s Drive

**Dimensions:** autonomy, mastery, purpose.

Best treated as a practical synthesis of SDT, work design, and meaning research, not a validated assessment.

### Amabile’s Progress Principle

**Core claim:** daily progress in meaningful work is a powerful driver of positive inner work life: emotion, motivation, and perceptions.

**Conditions:**

| Progress enablers | Progress blockers |
|---|---|
| clear goals, resources, autonomy, help, learning, enough time, open ideas | unclear goals, bureaucracy, setbacks without learning, resource starvation, disrespect, emotional toxins |

**Best elicitation questions:**

1. “What kind of progress feels meaningful to you: shipping, solving, learning, helping, winning, quality, risk reduction?”
2. “What small wins keep you going?”
3. “What makes a day feel wasted even if you were busy?”
4. “Where do you need autonomy most: task, time, technique, or team?”
5. “What purpose must be visible for the work to matter: user impact, craft excellence, social mission, team success, or business results?”

**Validity / criticism:** Pink is not psychometric and can oversimplify incentives. Rewards are not always bad; controlling rewards can undermine intrinsic motivation, but fair pay, informational feedback, and well-designed incentives matter. Amabile’s evidence is stronger as a work-design and management lens than as an individual-trait framework: progress is contextual, and people differ in which progress signals matter.

## 7. What Actually Predicts Job Satisfaction

**Sources:** Work design meta-analysis: https://doi.org/10.1037/0021-9010.92.5.1332; Person-environment fit meta-analysis: https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1744-6570.2005.00672.x; O*NET IP manual: https://www.onetcenter.org/dl_files/IP_Manual.pdf; Perceived organizational support meta-analysis: https://doi.org/10.1037/0021-9010.87.4.698

**Best-supported predictors to capture in an AI interview:**

| Predictor | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Person-job fit | match between abilities/interests/needs and actual job demands/supplies |
| Work design | autonomy, task variety, task identity, task significance, feedback, manageable complexity |
| Relationships | supervisor support, coworker support, trust, belonging, respect |
| Skill / strength use | chance to do what one does best and improve at it |
| Values fit | especially needs-supplies fit: what the job provides vs what the person actually needs |
| SDT need satisfaction | autonomy, competence, relatedness predict engagement and well-being |
| Progress in meaningful work | visible movement on work the person cares about |
| Interest congruence | useful, but usually not enough by itself |

**Practical interpretation:** interest fit helps identify promising work families; satisfaction depends heavily on job design and social context. A person with strong Investigative-Artistic interests may still hate a research job with no autonomy, poor feedback, weak relationships, or values conflict.

**Recommended AI interview output schema:**

| Profile field | Best source frameworks |
|---|---|
| Preferred work activities | RIASEC, O*NET Interest Profiler |
| Non-negotiable career motives | Schein anchors, values card sort |
| Strength patterns | CliftonStrengths-style behavioral strengths, VIA character strengths |
| Values and tradeoffs | Schwartz, card sort, O*NET Work Values |
| Engagement conditions | SDT, flow, work design |
| Progress signals | Amabile, Pink |
| Risk factors | anchor violations, value conflicts, low autonomy/competence/relatedness, weak feedback, poor relationship fit |
| Role hypotheses | map to O*NET tasks, not job titles only |
