# Career-Thinking Frameworks Research Brief

## 1. 80,000 Hours Career Guide
**Core claims**
- Fulfillment is less about “following passion” and more about getting good at something useful to others. Current guide lists five dream-job ingredients; older references to “six” can be reconstructed by splitting the final bucket into basic needs and life fit: engaging work, helping others, work you can become good at, supportive colleagues, basic needs, fit with the rest of life.
- “Follow your passion” fails because many people lack career-relevant passions, interests change, interest-job match weakly predicts satisfaction, and passion-heavy fields are often crowded.
- Career capital = skills/knowledge, connections, credentials/reputation, character, and runway. Early career often should invest in this, but not through generic prestige by default.
- Personal fit = expected performance versus the typical person after serious effort. Because output is highly skewed, fit can multiply impact, satisfaction, and capital.
- Exploration value = information gained by trying an option. Best experiments are options that could be much better than your current best guess, are uncertain, and can be tested cheaply.
- Decision process: generate options, compare on 3-7 factors, investigate uncertainties, check gut signals, then make an A/B/Z plan and review every 6-24 months.

**Actionable exercises / interview questions**
- Score each career option 1-5 on engagement, helping others, skill fit, colleagues, basic needs/life fit.
- Ask: When have you been most fulfilled? What did those episodes have in common? What job would you try if money were no object or you had 10 years left?
- Personal-fit ladder: read 2-5 hours, talk to insiders 2-20 hours, do a 1-4 week project, then try internships/graduate study/jobs.
- Decision prompts: What decision is actually due? What 3-7 factors matter? What options are you missing? How are you most likely wrong? What would change your ranking?
- Review prompts: What have I learned since the last plan? Does it change my vision or next step? If making a new plan today, what would it be? Are concrete alternatives now maybe better?

**Evidence strength**
- Stronger: job characteristics model, autonomy/feedback/task variety, social support, job insecurity, commute, structured decision-making, and weak interest-match evidence.
- Moderate: altruism/meaning links, career capital/expertise development, cheap tests, exploration value.
- Weaker/speculative: cause prioritization, longtermist impact claims, forecasting high-impact paths.

**Criticisms**
- Normatively shaped by effective altruism and high-impact careers; less neutral than it appears.
- Best suited to educated, mobile people with options; weaker for constrained labor markets.
- Can overcorrect against passion: intrinsic interest still helps persistence.
- “Impact” and “helping others” are morally contested and culturally specific.

Sources: [dream job](https://80000hours.org/career-guide/dream-job/), [job satisfaction research](https://80000hours.org/articles/job-satisfaction-research/), [personal fit](https://80000hours.org/career-guide/personal-fit/), [career capital](https://80000hours.org/career-guide/career-capital/), [career decisions](https://80000hours.org/career-guide/career-decisions/), [career planning](https://80000hours.org/career-guide/career-planning/), [career exploration](https://80000hours.org/articles/career-exploration/)

## 2. Cal Newport, *So Good They Can’t Ignore You*
**Core claims**
- Passion mindset asks “What can the world offer me?” and breeds dissatisfaction; craftsman mindset asks “What can I offer the world?” and focuses attention on quality.
- Career capital theory: great work traits such as control, creativity, impact, and autonomy are rare and valuable, so you need rare and valuable skills to obtain them.
- Control is desirable only after enough capital. Control trap 1: seeking autonomy before being valuable. Control trap 2: once valuable, employers resist giving autonomy.
- Mission comes after mastery, not before. A compelling mission usually appears at the “adjacent possible” of a field once you understand its frontier.
- Use small bets to test missions and the “law of financial viability” to test whether the market values a move.

**Actionable exercises / interview questions**
- What rare and valuable skills have you built? What proof exists?
- Where are you using a passion mindset rather than a craftsman mindset?
- What deliberate-practice loop do you have: stretch goal, feedback, repetition, visible score?
- What autonomy do you want, and have you earned enough capital for it?
- What small mission bets could you run in 30-90 days?

**Evidence strength**
- Moderate conceptual evidence from self-determination theory, deliberate practice, skill acquisition, and later interest-development research.
- Low as a career intervention: largely narrative, case-study driven, and not experimentally validated as a whole framework.

**Criticisms**
- May underweight intrinsic interest, values, identity, and emotional signals.
- “Financial viability” can bias toward market-valued work over socially valuable or artistic work.
- Examples risk survivorship bias.
- Less useful for workers with low autonomy, discrimination, caregiving constraints, or limited safety net.

Sources: [Cal Newport books page](https://calnewport.com/writing/), [Newport on passion](https://calnewport.com/on-passion-and-its-discontents/), [Hachette book page](https://www.hachettebookgroup.com/titles/cal-newport/so-good-they-cant-ignore-you/9781455509126/), [O’Keefe/Dweck/Walton passion study](https://doi.org/10.1177/0956797618780643)

## 3. Gardner, Csikszentmihalyi & Damon, *Good Work*
**Core claims**
- Good work is the intersection of the 3 Es: excellence, engagement, and ethics.
- Excellence: work meets high standards of the domain or profession.
- Engagement: work is absorbing, meaningful, identity-relevant, often close to flow.
- Ethics: work serves responsibilities beyond self-interest, including clients, colleagues, communities, and society.
- Alignment matters: good work becomes easier when personal values, professional/domain standards, and institutional incentives reinforce each other. Misalignment creates ethical drift and compromised work.

**Actionable exercises / interview questions**
- Excellence: What standards define great work here? Who judges? What craft do you want to master?
- Engagement: Which tasks absorb you? Which feel deadening? When do you lose track of time?
- Ethics: Who is affected by your work? What obligations come with doing it well? What lines would you not cross?
- Alignment map: personal values, domain standards, organizational incentives. Where do they align, conflict, or stay ambiguous?
- Dilemma prompt: Describe a time doing excellent work conflicted with incentives, loyalty, or ethics. What did you choose and why?

**Evidence strength**
- Moderate as qualitative theory: grounded in interviews with professionals and strong links to flow, professional ethics, and identity research.
- Low as a validated intervention: not a predictive scoring model or RCT-backed career-choice tool.

**Criticisms**
- Ethical standards are plural and contested.
- Original work emphasizes professions and elites more than precarious or low-power workers.
- “Alignment” can be rare; the framework diagnoses conflicts better than it solves structural incentives.
- Engagement, excellence, and ethics can conflict rather than harmonize.

Sources: [The Good Project](https://www.thegoodproject.org/), [Project Zero Good Project](https://pz.harvard.edu/projects/the-good-project), [book page](https://www.basicbooks.com/titles/howard-gardner/good-work/9780465026081/), [Csikszentmihalyi publication listing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mihaly_Csikszentmihalyi)

## 4. Amy Wrzesniewski: Work Orientations & Job Crafting
**Core claims**
- People relate to work as a job, career, or calling. Job orientation emphasizes pay/security; career orientation emphasizes advancement/status; calling orientation emphasizes meaning, identity, and contribution.
- Same occupation can contain all three orientations; meaning is not fixed by job title.
- Job crafting: workers actively reshape task boundaries, relational boundaries, and cognitive meaning.
- Task crafting: change what work you do, how much, sequence, scope, or emphasis.
- Relational crafting: change who you interact with, how often, and in what way.
- Cognitive crafting: reframe what the work means and who it serves.

**Actionable exercises / Job Crafting Exercise mechanics**
- Map current job as building blocks: tasks, relationships, time, energy, attention.
- Identify values, strengths, motives, and passions.
- Redesign the map: add, drop, shrink, expand, delegate, reorder, or reframe tasks and relationships.
- Convert redesign into small experiments and commitments.
- Interview questions: What parts of your job would you expand if trusted? Which relationships make work meaningful? What story makes the work worth doing?

**Evidence strength**
- Moderate: job/career/calling orientations show correlational links with satisfaction and meaning; job crafting has theoretical, survey, diary, and meta-analytic support.
- Intervention evidence is promising but thinner. The Job Crafting Exercise reports a Fortune 500 quasi-experiment with improved happiness/effectiveness six weeks later.

**Criticisms**
- Can individualize structural problems: bad pay, bad management, unsafe work, and discrimination cannot be crafted away.
- Calling language can enable exploitation and burnout.
- Crafting may help the individual while burdening coworkers or conflicting with organizational needs.
- Requires autonomy; less available in tightly controlled jobs.

Sources: [Job Crafting Exercise](https://positiveorgs.bus.umich.edu/cpo-tools/job-crafting-exercise/), [Crafting a Job](https://doi.org/10.5465/amr.2001.4378011), [Jobs, Careers, and Callings](https://doi.org/10.1006/jrpe.1997.2162), [HBR job crafting article](https://hbr.org/2010/06/managing-yourself-turn-the-job-you-have-into-the-job-you-want), [job crafting meta-analysis](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jvb.2017.05.008)

## 5. Herminia Ibarra, *Working Identity*
**Core claims**
- Career change is not mainly an introspective search for the “true self.” Identity changes through action, social feedback, and revised stories.
- Reflection alone fails because old identities, old networks, and old success metrics keep people stuck.
- Three practices drive transition: experimenting with possible selves, shifting connections, and making sense through narrative.
- Experiments should be small, real, and socially exposed: side projects, temporary assignments, moonlighting, volunteering, courses, advisory roles, or consulting.
- New connections matter because old networks often reinforce the old identity; weak ties and new communities provide information, models, and permission.
- Coherent identity stories often come after experiments, not before.

**Actionable exercises / interview questions**
- List 3-5 possible selves, not one ideal job.
- For each possible self, design a low-cost test: activity, people, setting, success signal, exit condition.
- Network map: who reinforces your old identity, who represents a possible new one, and which weak ties can open doors?
- Sensemaking prompts: What did the experiment teach about energy, competence, credibility, values, and market pull? What story are you now telling about the change?
- Avoid the big-bang leap unless necessary; prefer iterative prototypes with accumulating evidence.

**Evidence strength**
- Moderate-low: strong qualitative grounding and fit with identity theory, possible-selves research, weak-tie/network theory, and experiential learning.
- Not a validated algorithm; strongest as a transition-process model for mid-career professionals.

**Criticisms**
- Based heavily on relatively privileged professionals with room to experiment.
- Can underplay financial risk, credential barriers, labor-market discrimination, and family constraints.
- Experimentation can become avoidance if not paired with decision points.
- Reflection is still necessary; the criticism is of reflection without contact with reality.

Sources: [HBR: How to Stay Stuck in the Wrong Career](https://hbr.org/2002/12/how-to-stay-stuck-in-the-wrong-career), [Working Identity book](https://herminiaibarra.com/working-identity/), [HBR author page](https://hbr.org/search?term=herminia%20ibarra), [weak ties research](https://doi.org/10.1086/225469)
